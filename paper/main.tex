\title{Stablecoin StressBench: An Execution-Aware Benchmark\\
       for Stablecoin Dislocations}

%% ---- Abstract -----------------------------------------------
\begin{abstract}
Quoted stablecoin price gaps are not trades.
We distinguish between \emph{optical} dislocations, where a quoted
basis exceeds a threshold, and \emph{executable} ones, where net
profit survives VWAP slippage, taker fees, and settlement latency
after routing through a live order book.
Building Stablecoin StressBench on the March~2023 USDC/SVB event,
we find that 34.3\% of minutes show a $>10\bps$ optical dislocation
while only 2.88\% are executable, yielding a $12\times$
price-to-execution gap (block bootstrap 95\,\% CI:
$8\times$--$24\times$), with no calm-trained model closing it and
the hindsight oracle exceeding $+225\bps$ net on the executable
arbitrage task.
Cross-mechanism meta-labeling trained on Terra/LUNA achieves
$+82.5\bps$ on the SVB test split (50.8\% of oracle returns),
demonstrating that order-book execution microstructure transfers
across structurally distinct stress mechanisms.
A three-way RL diagnostic then establishes that the unconditioned
PPO-GRU degenerates to NoTrade at 2.3\% positive density, the
conditioned variant at 17\% density trades 919 times but earns only
$-29.2\bps$, and that only meta-labeling's explicit positive-label
supervision reaches $+82.5\bps$, showing that positive-label density
is necessary but not sufficient for positive returns.
\end{abstract}

\keywords{stablecoin arbitrage, market microstructure,
  execution-aware labels, financial benchmarks, cryptocurrency}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

During stablecoin stress events, peg fragmentation creates cross-venue
price gaps that appear to be arbitrage opportunities.
In practice, VWAP slippage, taker fees, and CEX-to-CEX settlement
latency jointly eliminate most apparent profit.
We call dislocations where the quoted basis exceeds a threshold
\emph{optical}, and those where net profit survives execution costs
\emph{executable}.

Prior machine-learning work on stablecoin predictability uses
price-based labels to define ``profitable'' windows, which conflates
the optical and executable layers and systematically overstates how
predictable actual trading opportunities are.
Stablecoin StressBench is designed to close this gap.
It provides per-minute execution-aware labels that separate three
dislocation layers: an \emph{optical} dislocation where the quoted
basis exceeds a threshold, an \emph{executable} one where net profit
is positive after VWAP execution and transaction costs at a specified
notional, and a \emph{predictable} one where a model correctly
identifies the window ex ante.
The distance between these layers is the paper's core measurement.

We build the benchmark on the March 2023 USDC/SVB stress event, where
Silicon Valley Bank's collapse triggered a brief USDC depeg.
Using Binance L2 order-book data\footnote{The BTC-USDC buy leg uses
kline-proxy depth for the SVB window; all other legs use real L2.
The 20\% depth haircut sensitivity in \S\ref{sec:gap} bounds this
approximation.} and a full VWAP book-walking model, we find that
34.3\% of 1-minute windows show a $>10\bps$ price basis but only
2.88\% remain net-profitable at \$10K notional: a 12$\times$
price-to-execution gap.
The headline result is robust to buy-leg proxy error: a 20\%
depth haircut moves the executable rate from 2.88\% to 2.40\%
and widens the ratio from $12\times$ to $14\times$, with the oracle
remaining above $+130\bps$.
We then construct a model ladder spanning rule-based thresholds,
statistical baselines, gradient-boosted classifiers, sequence models
(GRU), and meta-labeling filters, evaluating each model's ability to
identify the 2.88\% of executable windows ex ante.
Every model trained on calm-control data loses money on executable
arbitrage tasks, and the hindsight oracle gap exceeds 260\,bps.
Cross-mechanism meta-labeling, trained on a different stress event
(Terra/LUNA 2022), is the only approach that achieves positive net
returns ($+82.5\bps$, 50.8\% oracle capture).

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt,topsep=2pt]
  \item An execution-aware benchmark with per-minute labels, documented
        order-book depth provenance, VWAP book-walking, fees, and a
        hindsight oracle ceiling, accompanied by an 18-event stress
        taxonomy serving as data-tier governance and an oracle gap
        decomposition into false-positive, timing, and sizing components.
        (\S\ref{sec:labels}, \S\ref{sec:historical};
         Tables~\ref{tab:gap}, \ref{tab:tiers}, \ref{tab:oracle})
  \item Cross-mechanism transfer evidence: meta-labeling trained on
        Terra/LUNA achieves $+82.5\bps$ (50.8\% oracle capture) on
        the SVB test split, which to our knowledge is the first
        demonstration that order-book execution microstructure
        transfers across structurally distinct stress mechanisms, on
        the single cross-mechanism pair with route-complete test data.
        (\S\ref{sec:diagnostics}, Table~\ref{tab:metalabel})
  \item An RL diagnostic establishing that positive-label density is
        necessary but not sufficient: a conditioned PPO-GRU at 17\%
        density earns $-29.2\bps$ versus meta-labeling's $+82.5\bps$
        at identical density, isolating explicit positive-label
        supervision as the additional requirement.
        (\S\ref{sec:rl_results}, Table~\ref{tab:rl_results})
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

The literature most relevant to our work spans stablecoin stress
events, limits to arbitrage, and financial machine learning.

Prior work on stablecoin stress provides the empirical backdrop for
our benchmark.
Uhlig \cite{uhlig2022luna} analyses the Terra/LUNA collapse, whose
algorithmic redemption spiral provides a structurally distinct
trigger from the SVB bank shock, making it a clean out-of-distribution
test for cross-mechanism transfer.
Griffin and Shams \cite{griffin2020tether} document that quoted
prices can reflect non-fundamental demand, motivating our separation
of optical from executable dislocations.
Hernandez Cruz et al.\ \cite{hernandezcruz2024svb} study USDC-USDT
DEX liquidity during the SVB collapse and confirm the importance of
execution-layer analysis.

The limits-to-arbitrage literature establishes the theoretical
foundations that our labels operationalise.
Shleifer and Vishny \cite{shleifer1997limits} and Gromb and Vayanos
\cite{gromb2010limits} show that capital constraints and execution
frictions bound arbitrage even when price gaps are observed.
Makarov and Schoar \cite{makarov2020trading} demonstrate that
cross-venue crypto price gaps are not executable profits once latency
and book depth are accounted for, directly motivating our $12\times$
price-to-execution gap.
Hautsch et al.\ \cite{hautsch2018blockchain} quantify settlement
latency as a first-order cost, which we implement as the fixed
$\delta{=}5\bps$ penalty.
Cont and Kukanov \cite{cont2013optimal} establish VWAP execution
price as the correct profitability object, which our book-walking
model in \S\ref{sec:netlabels} implements directly.
Gogol et al.\ \cite{gogol2024amm} introduce a Maximal Arbitrage
Value between a USDC-ETH AMM pool and Binance, and our oracle
extends this to route-level CEX simulation and model-evaluation
benchmarking.

The financial machine learning literature informs both our methodology
and our positioning.
L\'{o}pez de Prado \cite{lopezdeprado2018} introduces meta-labeling
as a technique for filtering a primary signal with a secondary model
that learns the conditions under which the primary signal is reliable.
We extend this architecture to the cross-mechanism setting in
\S\ref{sec:diagnostics}, training on Terra/LUNA and evaluating on
SVB, a transfer that to our knowledge has not been attempted before.
Cintra and Holloway \cite{cintra2023bocpd} apply BOCPD
\cite{adamsmackay2007bocpd} to detect the Terra depeg from Curve AMM
pool reserves and demonstrate cross-event transfer via AMM depletion
signals. However, their signals differ fundamentally from CEX
cross-quote basis, which is noisier and mean-reverting, and no oracle
evaluation is reported.
On reinforcement learning, Moallemi and Wang \cite{moallemi2022rl}
and Ning et al.\ \cite{ning2021ddql} establish the finite-horizon MDP
framing for optimal execution that we adopt in \S\ref{sec:rl_baseline}.
Mohl et al.\ \cite{jaxmarlhft2024} demonstrate the feasibility of
GPU-scale multi-agent RL on production limit-order-book data, training
IPPO on approximately 400 million orders for a joint execution and
market-making task.
Olby et al.\ \cite{rightplace2024} construct a reactive Simudyne
simulator that decomposes execution costs into market impact and
execution risk, benchmarking RL timing policies against an
Almgren--Chr\'iss efficient frontier.
Both works confirm that RL can learn effective execution policies
when the environment is reactive. Our benchmark contributes the
complementary finding that in non-reactive historical replay with
structurally rare positive labels, positive-label density and explicit
supervision are necessary preconditions.
No prior stablecoin-stress benchmark provides one-minute executable
labels, route-level depth and fee modelling, a hindsight oracle
ceiling, and cross-mechanism transfer evaluation jointly. StressBench
is the first to do so.

\section{Benchmark Design}
\label{sec:benchmark}

\subsection{Data Sources and Instruments}
\label{sec:data}

The final dataset contains 56,134 1-minute bars across six windows
(Table~\ref{tab:dataset}).
Price features come from Binance Vision (klines, aggTrades) and Coinbase
REST candles.
Net-profit labels use Binance BTC-USDC and BTC-USDT depth plus Coinbase
BTC-USD as the reference leg.

Every depth record is tagged with a provenance field
(Table~\ref{tab:route_provenance}).
The sell leg (BTC-USDT) uses real L2 futures bookDepth for all 2023--2024
windows.
The cross leg (USDC-USDT) uses real L2 from 2023-03-12 onward.
The buy leg (BTC-USDC) uses kline-proxy for the SVB window (BTC-USDC
perpetual did not exist on Binance until 2024) and real L2 for 2024
windows.
To bound this proxy error, we apply a 20\% depth haircut to the
kline-proxy buy leg. The executable rate falls from 2.88\% to 2.40\%,
the ratio widens from $12\times$ to $14\times$, and the oracle remains
above $+130\bps$, confirming that the headline result is robust to
buy-leg proxy error.

\subsection{Event-Based Split Design}
\label{sec:splits}

Splits are defined by event identity (Figure~\ref{fig:basis}).
No model trained on the train split has seen stress dynamics, and
threshold calibration on validation uses an independent stress event.
Labels are constructed by aligning each bar's outcome to the
observation window ending at $t{+}h$, with explicit checks confirming
no future information leaks into the input features.

\subsection{Historical Stress Taxonomy: Data-Tier Governance}
\label{sec:historical}

The 18-event taxonomy (2020--2023) serves as a benchmark governance
artefact specifying which events carry sufficient data quality for
execution-grade claims and which are restricted to price magnitude
estimates or mechanism classification only.
It is shipped with the benchmark to foreclose over-interpretation
of results, not as an empirical finding in its own right.
Seven mechanism classes are covered: \emph{algorithmic/reflexive}
(FEI, IRON/TITAN, MIM, UST, USDD); \emph{fiat-reserve bank shock}
(USDC/SVB); \emph{regulatory winddown} (BUSD); \emph{exchange credit}
(Celsius/3AC, HUSD, FTX \cite{vidalthomas2023ftx}); \emph{DeFi pool
imbalance} (Curve/UST, USDT/Curve, USDC/DAI); \emph{collateral
liquidation} (DAI Black Thursday); \emph{RWA/niche} (Acala~aUSD, USDR).

Data availability governs permitted claims (Table~\ref{tab:tiers}).
Tier-A events (USDC/SVB test) support execution-gap, oracle-gap,
and model evaluation claims.
Tier-B ($N{=}11$, OHLCV/pool data) support price magnitude estimates
only.
Tier-C ($N{=}5$, partial/DEX-only) support mechanism taxonomy only.

\section{Execution-Aware Labels}
\label{sec:labels}

Labels operationalise the three-layer framework introduced in \S\ref{sec:intro}.
The price-to-execution gap measures Layer~1 divided by Layer~2.
The oracle gap measures Layer~3 against Layer~2 in hindsight.
This section details label construction.

\subsection{Cross-Quote Basis and Labels}
\label{sec:basis}

\[
  b_{\text{USDC}}(t) = 10{,}000 \times
    \frac{P_{\text{BTC/USDC}}(t) - P_{\text{BTC/USD}}(t)}
         {P_{\text{BTC/USD}}(t)} \quad [\text{bps}]
\]
$b_{\max} = \max(|b_{\text{USDC}}|, |b_{\text{USDT}}|)$.
Basis label: $y_{\text{basis}}(t) = \mathbf{1}[|b_{\text{USDC}}(t{+}h)| > \tau]$.
Primary task: \task{basis\_usdc\_1m\_gt10bps}.

\subsection{VWAP Execution and Net Profit Labels}
\label{sec:netlabels}

\begin{align}
  \text{net}(q,t) &= 10{,}000 \times
    \left[\frac{\text{VWAP}_{\text{sell}} - \text{VWAP}_{\text{buy}}}{P_{\text{ref}}}
    - f_{\text{buy}} - f_{\text{sell}} - \delta_{\text{settle}}\right]
    \quad [\text{bps}]\\
  y_{\text{arb}}(q,h,t) &= \mathbf{1}\!\left[
    \max_{s \in [t,\,t+h]} \text{net}(q,s) > c \right]
\end{align}

Here $P_{\text{ref}}$ is the Coinbase BTC-USD reference price and
$f_{\text{buy}}, f_{\text{sell}}, \delta_{\text{settle}}$ are
dimensionless fractions representing per-leg taker fees and settlement
latency costs, respectively.
We set the profit threshold $c{=}10\bps$, taker fee to $4\bps$ per
side, and $\delta{=}5\bps$. The primary economic task is
\task{executable\_arb\_q10000\_5m}.
The hindsight oracle trades every minute where $\text{net}(q,t) > c$,
establishing a performance ceiling that no deployable model can exceed,
given that any real model must decide without access to future
information.

\section{Models and Evaluation}
\label{sec:models}

\subsection{Model Ladder}
\label{sec:stack}

The ladder is constructed so that each rung tests a distinct
hypothesis about the source of the oracle gap.
The economic anchors, \texttt{NoTrade} at $0\bps$ and the hindsight
\texttt{Oracle}, bracket the achievable range.
Price-threshold rules (\texttt{PriceBasis\{10,25\}bps},
\texttt{GrossArbThreshold}) test whether simple signal filters suffice.
Tabular classifiers (logistic regression, Lasso, Random Forest,
XGBoost, LightGBM) trained on four nested feature sets test whether
microstructure conditioning closes the gap.
An expected-net-profit regressor that directly targets
$\hat{y}{=}\text{net}(q,t)$ tests whether the gap is a
label-mismatch artefact, with failure implying it is structural.
A meta-labeling filter trains a secondary LightGBM model to learn
$\Pr[\text{net}{>}c \mid |b_{\text{USDC}}|{>}10\bps]$, while GRU and
MLP-Window sequence models trained on 30-minute sliding windows test
whether temporal depth patterns are exploitable
(\S\ref{sec:seq_results}).

\subsection{RL Execution Policy Baseline}
\label{sec:rl_baseline}

We frame the entry-timing problem as a finite-horizon MDP.
At each minute $t$ the agent observes state $s_t$, selects action
$a_t \in \{\text{enter},\text{wait}\}$, and receives reward
$r_t = \text{net}(q,t)$ if entering (positive for profitable windows,
negative otherwise) or $r_t = 0$ if waiting.
The state $s_t$ is the 30-minute look-back window of
\texttt{price\_plus\_book} features, the smallest feature set that
delivers meaningful microstructure lift over price-only signals
(see \S\ref{sec:shap} for SHAP evidence).
This feature set is available in real time without
settlement-latency dependency.
We train a PPO agent with a GRU policy network (one hidden layer,
64 units, 30-step look-back) using the cross-mechanism protocol,
training on Terra/LUNA stress windows only and evaluating on the
USDC/SVB test split without retraining.
This directly tests whether the sequential timing framing resolves
the dominant gap component identified in \S\ref{sec:decomp}.

\subsection{Calibration and Evaluation}
\label{sec:eval}

Thresholds are calibrated on the validation split by maximising
$\theta^* = \arg\max_\theta \sum_{i:\hat{p}_i>\theta} \text{net}(q,t_i)$
subject to a minimum of 25 trades.
The primary metrics are net bps captured and oracle capture percentage,
defined as model net bps divided by oracle net bps (the fraction of
the oracle's per-trade return that a model recovers).
Secondary metrics are AUROC and AUPRC.
\texttt{NoTrade} at $0\bps$ is the economic floor.

All results are reported on the SVB test split ($N{=}15{,}832$) with
the cost parameters stated above; the kline-proxy approximation on
the BTC-USDC buy leg is bounded by the haircut sensitivity in
\S\ref{sec:gap}.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Price Gaps Are Common, Execution Is Rare}
\label{sec:gap}

At a 10\,bps threshold, 34.3\% of test minutes exceed the max-basis
criterion while only 2.88\% yield net profit, a $12\times$
price-to-execution ratio (block bootstrap 95\,\% CI:
$8\times$--$24\times$, $B{=}10{,}000$, 60-min blocks).
This gap persists at all tested thresholds (Table~\ref{tab:gap}) and
is robust across cost regimes. At the worst-case setting of 10\,bps
settlement and high fees, the executable rate falls to 2.40\% and
the ratio widens to $14\times$. The gap also deepens with notional
size, falling from 2.88\% at \$10K to 1.62\% at \$50K and 0.03\%
at \$500K, confirming that book depth rather than fees prices out
institutional participants, consistent with the limits-to-arbitrage
arguments in \S\ref{sec:related}.

\subsection{Cross-Mechanism Transfer: Benchmark Generalisation Evidence}
\label{sec:diagnostics}

When the meta-labeling filter is trained on the calm-control split,
it yields zero meta-positives by construction (0\% positive rate).
We therefore retrain on the Terra/LUNA validation split, which contains
1,559 primary fires and 265 meta-positives (17.0\% positive rate), and
evaluate cross-mechanism on the SVB test split.
All fitting and threshold selection use Terra/LUNA only; no SVB labels
are observed before final evaluation.
With \texttt{price\_plus\_book} features the filter achieves $+82.5\bps$
(397 trades, 50.8\% oracle capture) vs.\ zero with calm training
(Table~\ref{tab:metalabel}).
This finding is consistent with execution microstructure that
transfers across stress mechanisms. The same order-book features that
predict profitable windows during an algorithmic collapse (Terra/LUNA)
also predict them during a fiat-reserve shock (SVB), because CEX
market makers withdraw depth under uncertainty regardless of the
underlying trigger. The cause is opaque at the order-book timescale,
making depth withdrawal a consistent cross-mechanism precursor whose
feature signature transfers even as the magnitude differs by event
(5.9$\times$ for Terra/LUNA versus $12\times$ for SVB).
The meta-model's dominant features mirror the main SHAP ranking, with
ask-side depth at 10\,bps and bid-ask spread as the leading signals
in both the calm-trained LightGBM and the Terra-trained secondary
classifier.
We are not aware of prior work demonstrating cross-mechanism transfer
for execution-aware stablecoin models.

\subsubsection*{Regime detectors, false-positive diagnosis, and SHAP}
\label{sec:shap}

Three regime-detection filters were evaluated as two-stage pre-filters.
CUSUM achieves near-perfect recall (0.9995) but at an 83.5\% false
alarm rate, while EWMA reaches 86.9\% accuracy with 0.95\% recall
and a 0.65\% false alarm rate.
BOCPD \cite{adamsmackay2007bocpd} achieves AUROC~0.229 on CEX
cross-quote data, in contrast to Cintra and Holloway
\cite{cintra2023bocpd}, who detected the Terra depeg 12 hours early
from Curve pool reserves. AMM reserves accumulate gradually, whereas
CEX cross-quote basis is mean-reverting, which explains why
changepoint detection is unreliable on exchange prices.

A structural diagnosis of false positives reveals that the dominant
failure mode of price-threshold rules is route-direction mismatch
rather than thin books.
\texttt{PriceBasis10bps} produces 2,002 trades, of which 581 (29\%)
are false positives at a mean cost of $-38.7\bps$ each.
True-positive windows carry a mean USDC basis of 344\,bps, reflecting
a genuine discount, whereas false-positive windows carry just
0.56\,bps and are triggered by USDT dislocations where the USDC route
is not simultaneously profitable.
Notably, false-positive windows exhibit \emph{higher} average bid depth
($72{,}805$ vs.\ $59{,}961$ BTC units) than true positives, ruling out
liquidity as the cause.
LightGBM's 33\% hit rate (compared to 6\% for the rule) demonstrates
that microstructure conditioning partially addresses this mismatch,
though a 67\% false-positive rate persists.

SHAP analysis identifies price features as carrying 94.8\% of total
importance in LightGBM, with ask-side depth and bid-ask spread
contributing just 3.4\% combined.
The causal interpretation is straightforward. A model trained on
calm-market data learns calm price dynamics rather than the
depth-withdrawal signature of stress events, so the microstructure
features are present in the input but rendered uninformative by the
training distribution. This explains why the oracle gap is structural
rather than a label-mismatch artefact, and why cross-mechanism training
on Terra/LUNA resolves it. The meta-model encounters stress
microstructure at 17\% positive density and learns the
depth-withdrawal signature that calm training cannot recover.

\subsection{Terra/LUNA Replication: Pattern Generality}
\label{sec:stress_recurrence}

The benchmark design principle generalises across stress mechanisms.
The Terra/LUNA validation split ($N{=}11{,}526$, May~2022) uses
kline-proxy depth (Tier-B) but replicates the core pattern, with
13.5\% price-basis above $10\bps$, only 2.30\% executable, and a
$5.9\times$ ratio versus $12\times$ for SVB. The oracle earns
$+88\bps$ on Terra/LUNA compared to $+225.0\bps$ on the SVB
executable arbitrage task, consistent with cross-mechanism
transferability of execution structure.
Among broader Tier-B events, peak optical gaps range from
approximately $100\bps$ (Celsius/3AC, exchange credit) to above
$500\bps$ (Curve/UST, DeFi pool imbalance) and above $800\bps$
(HUSD issuer failure), all estimated from OHLCV. Execution-grade
claims require route-complete L2 and are therefore reserved for

\subsection{Oracle Gap Decomposition}
\label{sec:decomp}

The 260\,bps gap on the executable arbitrage task decomposes into
three components, with the first derived directly from test-split
results and the remaining two estimated from the executable-rate
sensitivity analysis.
The dominant and directly measurable component is false-positive cost.
For \texttt{PriceBasis10bps}, 581 false-positive trades at $-38.7\bps$
each produce $-22{,}484\bps$ of total losses, which represents the
primary quantifiable drag for any threshold-based strategy.
The second component is timing error (estimated 40--80\,bps), arising
because entering 1--2 minutes late shifts execution to a lower-depth
regime. The depth-withdrawal signature in the 30-minute pre-window is
the signal a future model must learn to anticipate.
The third component is sizing error (estimated 40--100\,bps), since
the executable rate falls from 2.88\% at \$10K to 1.62\% at \$50K,
pricing out 44\% of windows above the retail ceiling.
Taken together, these components suggest that closing the gap requires
cross-mechanism stress training combined with earlier timing entry
rather than larger notional size.

\subsection{RL Diagnostic: Density Is Necessary but Not Sufficient}
\label{sec:rl_results}

The sharpest architectural finding of this paper is that explicit
positive-label supervision beats RL reward optimisation by $111.7\bps$
at identical training density (17\%). The three-way ladder below
establishes density as necessary but explicit supervision as
additionally required.

Terra/LUNA stress windows and applied cross-mechanism to the USDC/SVB

The 2.30\% positive rate in the full Terra/LUNA stream produces a
reward signal too sparse to calibrate entry timing; the agent's
expected reward from entering is negative, so it abstains.

A conditioned PPO-GRU, trained only on primary-signal windows
($|b_{\text{USDC}}|{>}10\bps$, mirroring the meta-label training
distribution at 17\% positive rate), does trade: it produces
919 entries with AUROC~0.87 and net $-29.2\bps$
(Table~\ref{tab:rl_results}), ruling out training sparsity as the
sole failure mode.
However, the conditioned agent cannot match meta-labeling
($+82.5\bps$, a gap of 111.7\,bps), indicating that explicit
positive-label supervision outperforms RL reward optimisation at
identical density.
The distinction is qualitative. The meta-labeling classifier receives
direct binary supervision, with $y{=}1$ for profitable windows, while
the RL agent must infer the profitable subset through a reward signal
that is negative on roughly 83\% of entries. The supervision is
therefore categorically cleaner, not merely denser.
The 919-trade overtrading pattern is itself diagnostic, since policies
that fail due to insufficient training typically converge toward
NoTrade rather than systematic false-positive entry. This suggests
that the failure reflects a timing-transfer mismatch rather than a
fundamental limitation of RL, and that multi-mechanism training is
the productive next step.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

Several limitations bound the scope of our claims.
On data, the BTC-USDC buy leg relies on kline-proxy depth for the SVB
window; the 20\% depth haircut sensitivity analysis confirms that this
does not materially alter the headline results.
Extending Tier-A coverage to AMM or exchange-credit events would
require route-complete L2 archives for those venues, which are not
currently available.

On the model side, the conditioned RL result ($-29.2\bps$, 919 trades)
may reflect overfitting to Terra/LUNA's specific depth-withdrawal
timing rather than a fundamental limitation of sequential optimisation.
Disentangling these alternatives requires additional cross-mechanism
pairs or extended training.
A further constraint is the absence of market-impact feedback. Unlike
reactive-simulator approaches such as Olby et al.\ \cite{rightplace2024},
where agents observe how their own order flow depletes book depth, the
PPO-GRU is trained on historical replay and cannot model this loop.
In a thin-book stress regime this systematically miscalibrates entry
sizing, and we regard it as a plausible contributor to the 919-trade
overtrading pattern at identical training density to meta-labeling.
Integrating the cross-mechanism training protocol developed here into
a reactive simulator represents the most direct path to resolving the
timing component of the oracle gap.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

The central finding of Stablecoin StressBench is that execution
microstructure is the binding constraint on stablecoin arbitrage,
not price signal quality.
A 34.3\% optical dislocation rate collapses to 2.88\% once order-book
depth and transaction costs are applied.
No model trained on calm-market data closes this gap. Cross-mechanism
training on a different stress event is the empirically validated path
to positive net returns ($+82.5\bps$, 50.8\% oracle capture).
No calm-control-trained model earns positive returns on the executable
arbitrage task. LightGBM ($+17.2\bps$, basis task only) is the sole
non-oracle positive result, remaining 145\,bps below the oracle
ceiling. The oracle gap decomposition (\S\ref{sec:decomp}) identifies
false-positive cost as the dominant quantifiable drag and timing as
the primary structural barrier.

The RL experiments (\S\ref{sec:rl_results}) sharpen this implication
by establishing that positive example density is necessary but not
sufficient, with explicit supervision of the positive subset as the
additional requirement.
The unconditioned PPO-GRU degenerates at 2.3\% density, which is too
sparse for the reward signal to calibrate entry timing.
The conditioned PPO-GRU trained on the same 17\% primary-signal
windows does trade ($-29.2\bps$, 919 trades) but cannot match
meta-labeling ($+82.5\bps$), leaving a 111.7\,bps gap attributable
to meta-labeling's explicit positive-label supervision rather than
RL reward optimisation.

To support reproducibility, the anonymised repository (URL redacted
for review) provides per-minute executable labels for all six benchmark
windows, the VWAP book-walking label-construction code with route-level
depth provenance, the four nested feature sets, the hindsight oracle
implementation and model-ladder harness, and the cross-mechanism
meta-labeling and PPO-GRU training scripts.
Calm-control and SVB windows are reproducible from the public Binance
Vision archive, while route-complete L2 requires a Tardis subscription.
