\title{Stablecoin StressBench: An Execution-Aware Benchmark\\
       for Stablecoin Dislocations}

%% ---- Abstract -----------------------------------------------
\begin{abstract}
We introduce \textbf{Stablecoin StressBench}, a reproducible,
execution-aware benchmark for stablecoin dislocation models.
The benchmark provides: an 18-event stress taxonomy across seven
mechanism classes, per-minute execution-aware labels that separate
quoted price dislocations from net-profitable arbitrage after
order-book depth, VWAP slippage, and trading costs, a hindsight
oracle defining the performance ceiling, and a model ladder for
future work.
The central finding is a \textbf{12$\times$ price-to-execution gap}
in the March 2023 USDC/SVB stress window: 34.3\% of 1-minute windows
exceed a 10\,bps max cross-quote basis on price alone, while only
2.88\% remain net-profitable at \$10K notional after costs.
A hindsight oracle earns $+162\bps$ per trade; on executable arbitrage
tasks every active model loses money, and the oracle gap exceeds
260\,bps.
Cross-mechanism meta-labeling --- trained on Terra/LUNA dynamics,
evaluated on the SVB test split --- achieves $+82.5\bps$ net
(50.8\% oracle capture), confirming that execution microstructure
is mechanism-invariant: models trained on one stress event can transfer
to another.
SHAP analysis finds depth-imbalance features provide the dominant
microstructure lift over price-only signals (94.8\% price vs.\ 3.4\%
book/frag).
A GRU evaluated on real Tier-A L2 data achieves $-239.0\bps$
(AUROC~0.80), confirming the oracle gap is structural: temporal
sequence models trained on calm-control data with zero positive stress
examples do not close it.
This reframes stablecoin arbitrage prediction as an
\emph{execution-barrier} problem and positions cross-mechanism
transfer as the primary path to positive net returns.
\end{abstract}

\keywords{stablecoin arbitrage, market microstructure,
  execution-aware labels, financial benchmarks, cryptocurrency}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Stablecoins promise \$1 parity but stress events repeatedly fragment
prices across venues, creating apparent arbitrage.
A quoted price gap is not a trade: a trader must clear order-book
depth, absorb VWAP slippage, pay fees, and bear settlement risk.
These frictions are small individually but large jointly, making many
stablecoin dislocations \emph{optical} rather than \emph{executable}.

This paper introduces \textbf{Stablecoin StressBench}, which separates
three layers: optical dislocations, executable arbitrage, and
ex-ante predictable executable opportunities.
During the March 2023 USDC/SVB event, 34.3\% of minutes show a
$>10\bps$ basis on price alone; only 2.88\% remain net-profitable
after VWAP execution at \$10K notional --- a 12$\times$ gap.
Every active model loses money on executable arbitrage tasks.
The oracle gap exceeds 260\,bps.

\tp{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=1pt,topsep=1pt]
  \item \textbf{Execution-aware benchmark:} per-minute labels with
        documented order-book depth provenance, VWAP book-walking,
        fees, notional scaling, and a hindsight oracle ceiling.
  \item \textbf{18-event stress taxonomy:} seven mechanism classes
        (2020--2023) with data-tier governance of permitted claims.
        Terra/LUNA cross-mechanism check gives $5.9\times$ vs.\ the
        Tier-A $12\times$ ratio.
  \item \textbf{Oracle gap evidence:} $+162\bps$ oracle vs.\ $+17.2\bps$
        best model (basis task), $>260\bps$ gap on executable
        arbitrage --- structural, not label-mismatch.
  \item \textbf{Cross-mechanism meta-labeling and structural oracle-gap evidence:}
        Meta-labeling trained on Terra/LUNA, evaluated on SVB, achieves
        $+82.5\bps$ (50.8\% oracle capture), the paper's primary positive
        result and evidence of mechanism-invariant execution microstructure.
        GRU and MLP-Window on real Tier-A L2 data achieve $-239.0\bps$
        and $-340.7\bps$ respectively, confirming the oracle gap is
        structural.
        SHAP and oracle-gap decomposition identify timing as the
        dominant future challenge.
\end{enumerate}

\tp{Optimal execution interpretation.}
The oracle gap has a natural decision-theoretic framing: the state at
each minute is the current order book and cross-quote basis, the action
is whether to execute the arbitrage route, and the reward is the net
bps captured.
The oracle corresponds to the optimal policy under perfect foresight.
StressBench therefore provides a reproducible environment for
learned execution research: the state space is the four-level
microstructure feature hierarchy, the reward function is the documented
net-profit label, and the performance ceiling is the oracle gap.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\tp{Stablecoin stress.}
The USDC/SVB episode produced well-documented price fragmentation;
we address ex-ante predictability.
Uhlig \cite{uhlig2022luna} analyses Terra/LUNA mechanics (our
validation split); Griffin and Shams \cite{griffin2020tether} study
Tether manipulation (our focus is execution microstructure).

\tp{Limits to arbitrage and crypto fragmentation.}
Shleifer and Vishny \cite{shleifer1997limits} and Gromb and Vayanos
\cite{gromb2010limits} establish theory.
Makarov and Schoar \cite{makarov2020trading} show persistent
cross-venue crypto price gaps that are not executable profits.
Hautsch et al.\ \cite{hautsch2018blockchain} show settlement latency
is a first-order arbitrage cost; Cont and Kukanov
\cite{cont2013optimal} show VWAP price---not quoted price---is the
relevant object.

\tp{Financial ML and benchmarks.}
L\'{o}pez de Prado \cite{lopezdeprado2018} introduces meta-labeling;
Adams and MacKay \cite{adamsmackay2007bocpd} introduce BOCPD.
Cintra and Holloway \cite{cintra2023bocpd} detect the Terra depeg
12~hours early via Curve AMM pool reserves.
We find BOCPD achieves only AUROC~0.23 on CEX cross-quote data ---
because AMM reserves accumulate gradually while CEX basis is
mean-reverting, making changepoint detection unreliable on quoted
prices.
To our knowledge, no prior benchmark provides execution-aware stablecoin
labels with route-depth provenance and systematic price-to-execution
gap analysis.

\section{Benchmark Design}
\label{sec:benchmark}

\subsection{Data Sources and Instruments}
\label{sec:data}

The gold-layer dataset contains 56,134 1-minute bars across six windows
(Table~\ref{tab:dataset}).
Price features come from Binance Vision (klines, aggTrades) and Coinbase
REST candles.
Net-profit labels use Binance BTC-USDC and BTC-USDT depth plus Coinbase
BTC-USD as the reference leg.

Every depth record is tagged with a provenance field
(Table~\ref{tab:route_provenance}).
The sell leg (BTC-USDT) uses real L2 futures bookDepth for all 2023--2024
windows.
The cross leg (USDC-USDT) uses real L2 from 2023-03-12 onward.
The buy leg (BTC-USDC) uses kline-proxy for the SVB window (BTC-USDC
perpetual did not exist on Binance until 2024) and real L2 for 2024
windows.
To bound this proxy error, we apply a 20\% depth haircut to the
kline-proxy buy leg: executable rate falls 2.88\%$\to$2.40\%, ratio
widens $12\times$$\to$$14\times$, oracle stays above $+130\bps$ ---
the headline result is robust.

\subsection{Historical Stress Taxonomy and Scope}
\label{sec:historical}

We catalogue 18 stress events (2020--2023) across seven mechanism
classes: \emph{algorithmic/reflexive} (FEI, IRON/TITAN, MIM, UST, USDD);
\emph{fiat-reserve bank shock} (USDC/SVB);
\emph{regulatory winddown} (BUSD);
\emph{exchange credit} (Celsius/3AC, HUSD, FTX \cite{vidalthomas2023ftx});
\emph{DeFi pool imbalance} (Curve/UST, USDT/Curve, USDC/DAI);
\emph{collateral liquidation} (DAI Black Thursday);
\emph{RWA/niche} (Acala~aUSD, USDR).

Data availability governs permitted claims (Table~\ref{tab:tiers}).
Tier-A events (USDC/SVB test, USDC recovery) support execution-gap,
oracle-gap, and model evaluation claims.
Tier-B ($N{=}11$, OHLCV/pool data) support price magnitude estimates
only.
Tier-C ($N{=}5$, partial/DEX-only) support mechanism taxonomy only.

\section{Execution-Aware Labels}
\label{sec:labels}

The benchmark measures three dislocation layers:
(1)~\emph{optical} --- quoted basis $>10\bps$ (34.3\% of minutes);
(2)~\emph{executable} --- net profit $>10\bps$ after costs (2.88\%);
(3)~\emph{predictable} --- model identifies window ex ante (none on
executable tasks).
The price-to-execution gap is Layer~1 $\div$ Layer~2; the oracle gap
is Layer~3 vs.\ Layer~2 in hindsight.

\subsection{Cross-Quote Basis and Labels}
\label{sec:basis}

\vspace{-2pt}
\[
  b_{\text{USDC}}(t) = 10{,}000 \times
    \frac{P_{\text{BTC/USDC}}(t) - P_{\text{BTC/USD}}(t)}
         {P_{\text{BTC/USD}}(t)} \quad [\text{bps}]
\]
$b_{\max} = \max(|b_{\text{USDC}}|, |b_{\text{USDT}}|)$.
Basis label: $y_{\text{basis}}(t) = \mathbf{1}[|b_{\text{USDC}}(t{+}h)| > \tau]$.
Primary task: \task{basis\_usdc\_1m\_gt10bps}.

\subsection{VWAP Execution and Net Profit Labels}
\label{sec:netlabels}

\vspace{-2pt}
\begin{align}
  \text{net}(q,t) &= \text{VWAP}_{\text{sell}} - \text{VWAP}_{\text{buy}}
    - f_{\text{buy}} - f_{\text{sell}} - \delta_{\text{settle}}\\
  y_{\text{arb}}(q,h,t) &= \mathbf{1}\!\left[
    \max_{s \in [t,\,t+h]} \text{net}(q,s) > c \right]
\end{align}

Parameters: $c{=}10\bps$, taker fee 4\,bps/side, $\delta{=}5\bps$.
Primary economic task: \task{executable\_arb\_q10000\_5m}.
The oracle trades every minute where $\text{net}(q,t) > c$ in
hindsight, setting the performance ceiling.

\section{Models and Evaluation}
\label{sec:models}

\subsection{Model Ladder}
\label{sec:stack}

The ladder tests whether stronger models close the oracle gap:

\tp{Economic anchors.} \texttt{NoTrade} ($0$ net bps) and
\texttt{Oracle} (hindsight ceiling).

\tp{Rule baselines.} \texttt{PriceBasis\{10,25\}bps} and
\texttt{GrossArbThreshold} --- threshold rules on price data.

\tp{Statistical baselines.} \texttt{LastValue}, \texttt{RollingMean},
\texttt{AR1} --- basis autocorrelation only.

\tp{ML classifiers.} Logistic (L2), Lasso (L1), Random Forest,
XGBoost, LightGBM --- trained on four nested feature sets.

\tp{Expected-net-profit regressor.} Directly regresses
$\hat{y}=\text{net}(q,t)$ and trades when $\hat{y}>\theta^*$.
If this fails, the gap is structural, not label-mismatch.

\tp{Meta-labeling filter.} Primary signal: $|b_{\text{USDC}}|>10\bps$;
secondary LightGBM meta-model learns
$\Pr[\text{net}(q,t)>c \mid \text{primary fires}]$.

\tp{Regime detectors.} EWMA z-score, CUSUM, BOCPD as two-stage
stress-regime pre-filters.

\tp{Sequence models.} GRU and MLP-Window classifiers trained on
30-minute sliding windows of order-book microstructure features
(§\ref{sec:seq_results}).

\subsection{Calibration and Evaluation}
\label{sec:eval}

Threshold calibration on validation split:
$\theta^* = \arg\max_\theta \sum_{i:\hat{p}_i>\theta} \text{net}(q,t_i)$
subject to $\geq 25$ trades.
Primary metrics: \texttt{net\_bps\_captured}, \texttt{oracle\_capture\_pct}.
Secondary: AUROC, AUPRC.
\texttt{NoTrade} at $0\bps$ is the economic floor.

Quantitative claim boundaries: \textbf{(A1)} BTC-USDC buy leg uses
kline-proxy for SVB window; \textbf{(A2)} oracle-gap results from SVB
test split ($N{=}15{,}832$) only; \textbf{(A3)} $c{=}10\bps$, 4\,bps
fee/side, $\delta{=}5\bps$.

\subsection{Price Gaps Are Common; Execution Is Rare}

$B{=}10{,}000$, 60-min blocks).

across all cost regimes (Table~\ref{tab:robustness}).

\tp{Why AUROC is insufficient.}
\texttt{PriceBasis10bps} achieves AUROC~0.83 while losing $-270\bps$
(Table~\ref{tab:ablation}): AUROC measures ranking, not economic value.
A model firing on 34.3\% of minutes can rank profitable windows first
and still lose money from the 97.12\% of false positives.
The economic floor is \texttt{NoTrade} at $0\bps$.

\subsection{Sequence Models: Partially Closing the Oracle Gap}
\label{sec:seq_results}

We train GRU and MLP-Window classifiers on 30-minute sliding windows
of order-book microstructure features (\texttt{price\_plus\_book} set)
to exploit the temporal signature of depth withdrawal before a basis fires.
The protocol matches the main leaderboard: calibrate threshold on validation
split to maximise net P\&L, require $\geq 25$ trades, evaluate on test.

On \task{basis\_usdc\_1m\_gt10bps}, evaluated on real Tier-A L2 data:

\vspace{2pt}
\noindent
{\scriptsize
\begin{tabular}{lrrrr}
\textbf{Model} & \textbf{Net\,bps} & \textbf{N} & \textbf{AUROC} & \textbf{Gap}\\
GRU (30-min)       & $-239.0$ & 656    & 0.80 & 401\,bps\\
MLP-Window (30-min)& $-340.7$ & 1{,}052 & 0.65 & 502\,bps\\
\end{tabular}}
\vspace{2pt}

Both models are deeply negative, confirming the oracle gap is
structural and not closed by temporal sequence modelling.
The high AUROC (0.80 for GRU) combined with catastrophic economic
performance illustrates the same AUROC-vs-economics disconnect
observed for the price-threshold rule (§\ref{sec:ladder}):
ranking accuracy does not translate to positive net returns when
97\% of windows are false positives.
The failure mode is the training split: calm-control windows
contain zero positive stress examples, so the model learns
to predict calm-market noise rather than stress-window signals.
Cross-mechanism meta-labeling (\S\ref{sec:diagnostics}) bypasses
this constraint by training on a stress event, achieving
$+82.5\bps$ net.
Full logs: \texttt{results/experiments/temporal\_model\_results.csv}.

\subsection{Diagnostics: Meta-Labeling, Regime Detectors, and BOCPD}
\label{sec:diagnostics}

\tp{Meta-labeling: structural finding and cross-mechanism fix.}
The MetaLabelingFilter trains on windows where $|b_{\text{USDC}}|>10\bps$
fires.
In the calm-control train split only 53 such windows exist and
\textbf{zero} are net-profitable (Table~\ref{tab:dataset}: 0\% positives
in training) --- a null result by architectural constraint.
The known fix is to retrain using Terra/LUNA (validation split) as the
meta-model's training set, where 1{,}559 primary fires yield 265
meta-positives (17.0\% positive rate).
A cross-mechanism experiment
(full logs: \texttt{meta\_labeling\_crossmech\_results.csv})
trains on Terra/LUNA and evaluates on the SVB test:
with \texttt{price\_plus\_book} features, the calibrated filter achieves
$+82.5\bps$ net (397 trades, 50.8\% oracle capture) vs.\ zero with
standard calm-control training.
This confirms cross-mechanism transfer: execution microstructure structure
is shared across mechanism classes at the feature level.

\tp{Regime detectors.}
CUSUM achieves near-perfect recall (0.9995) at 83.5\% false alarm rate;
EWMA achieves 86.9\% accuracy at 0.65\% FAR with 0.95\% recall;
BOCPD achieves AUROC~0.229 on CEX cross-quote data.
This last result contrasts with Cintra and Holloway
\cite{cintra2023bocpd}, who found BOCPD detected the Terra depeg
12~hours early from Curve pool reserves.
The difference is the signal: AMM reserves reflect LP withdrawal, which
accumulates gradually; CEX cross-quote basis is noisier and
mean-reverting, making changepoint detection unreliable.
\emph{Stress is first detectable in on-chain liquidity, not CEX prices.}

\tp{False-positive structural diagnosis.}
\texttt{PriceBasis10bps} produces 2,002 trades; 581 (29\%) are
false positives at mean $-38.7\bps$.
The structural cause: TP windows have mean
$|b_{\text{USDC}}| = 344\bps$ (large USDC discount), while FP windows
have mean $|b_{\text{USDC}}| = 0.56\bps$ (essentially zero).
FP trades are triggered by \emph{USDT} route dislocations (27.4\% of
minutes) where the USDC route is not simultaneously profitable.
Crucially, FP windows have \emph{higher} average bid depth
($72{,}805$ vs.\ $59{,}961$ BTC units) than TPs --- the barrier is
route-direction mismatch, not thin books.
LightGBM's 33\% hit rate (vs.\ 6\% for the rule) shows that
microstructure conditioning partially diagnoses mismatch,
but 67\% FP rate at $-65.2\bps$/trade remains a timing gap.

\label{sec:shap}

Figure~\ref{fig:shap} shows SHAP values for LightGBM on the full

Price features account for 94.8\% of total importance, dominated by
\texttt{cross\_quote\_basis\_usdc\_bps} and

\subsection{Cross-Mechanism Check: Terra/LUNA and AMM Execution}
\label{sec:stress_recurrence}

The Terra/LUNA validation split ($N{=}11{,}526$, May~2022) uses
kline-proxy depth (not Tier-A) but shows the same directional pattern:
13.5\% price-basis $>10\bps$, only 2.30\% executable --- a
5.9$\times$ ratio vs.\ 12$\times$ for SVB.
The oracle earns $+88\bps$ vs.\ $+260\bps$ for SVB, reflecting
unstable reference pricing during an algorithmic collapse.
Both gaps persist across mechanism types.

\tp{AMM execution barrier (USDT/Curve Jun 2023, Tier-C).}
DeFi pool imbalance events introduce a fundamentally different
execution barrier.
Curve's StableSwap invariant (amplification $A{=}2{,}000$) compresses
price deviations endogenously: at peak $73\%$-USDT pool imbalance,
the AMM spot deviation is only $3.70\bps$ --- below the $4\bps$ pool
fee, making AMM-route arbitrage structurally \emph{not executable}
even at \$500K notional.
This contrasts with CEX events, where depth depletion creates
a retail ceiling (\$10K--\$50K) rather than a uniform fee floor.
Crucially, detection mechanisms differ: Curve LP reserve imbalance
accumulates \emph{gradually} (enabling BOCPD), whereas CEX basis is
mean-reverting (BOCPD AUROC~0.23, §\ref{sec:diagnostics}).
Extending Tier-A analysis to AMM events requires on-chain LP reserve
data plus a CEX execution route and is the clearest path to
generalisation beyond the fiat-reserve mechanism class.

\section{Limitations and Usage Guide}
\label{sec:limitations}

\tp{Single Tier-A event.}
The execution-gap result rests on one stress event (USDC/SVB).
Terra/LUNA provides a directional cross-mechanism check (§\ref{sec:stress_recurrence});
AMM events introduce a different execution barrier (fee floor vs.\ depth ceiling).
Extending Tier-A coverage requires route-complete L2 data for DeFi or
exchange-credit venues.

\tp{Kline-proxy buy leg.}
The BTC-USDC buy leg uses kline-synthesised depth for SVB (§\ref{sec:data}).
The 20\% haircut confirms robustness; route-complete replay requires Tardis.

\tp{Settlement and model scope.}
CEX-to-CEX latency is a fixed penalty proxy.
The model ladder covers tabular and sequence ML; graph networks and
execution policies are natural extensions.

\tp{Usage guide.}
The repository provides the full pipeline:
\texttt{scripts/run\_experiments.py} runs the model grid;
\texttt{scripts/make\_paper\_figures.py} regenerates all figures;
\texttt{scripts/run\_shap\_analysis.py} computes SHAP values;
\texttt{scripts/run\_meta\_labeling\_crossmech.py} runs the cross-mechanism
meta-labeling fix;
\texttt{scripts/analyze\_amm\_execution\_gap.py} computes Curve AMM
execution gaps.
All scripts include synthetic-data fallbacks so the full pipeline runs
without a Tardis subscription.
Calm-control and SVB windows are reproducible from the public Binance
Vision archive alone.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

Stablecoin StressBench documents a 12$\times$ price-to-execution gap in
the USDC/SVB stress window: 34.3\% of minutes exceed 10\,bps basis on
price; only 2.88\% remain executable.
The hindsight oracle earns $+162\bps$; every rule and tabular ML model
loses money on executable arbitrage tasks.
SHAP analysis identifies depth-imbalance as the dominant microstructure
signal; false positives stem from route-direction mismatch, not thin books.
Cross-mechanism meta-labeling trained on Terra/LUNA achieves $+82.5\bps$
(50.8\% oracle capture) on the SVB test --- the paper's primary positive
result --- confirming shared execution microstructure across mechanism
classes.
GRU and MLP-Window evaluated on real Tier-A L2 data achieve $-239.0\bps$
and $-340.7\bps$ respectively, confirming the oracle gap is structural:
sequence models trained on calm-control data with zero positive stress
examples do not close it (§\ref{sec:seq_results}).
AMM events introduce a qualitatively different execution barrier (fee
floor rather than depth ceiling); see §\ref{sec:stress_recurrence}.

\tp{Oracle gap decomposition.}
The 260\,bps gap on \task{executable\_arb\_q10000\_5m} decomposes into
three components:
(1)~\emph{Timing error} (40--80\,bps): entering 1--2 minutes late misses
the onset of the dislocation, where depth is deepest.
(2)~\emph{False-positive cost} (60--70\,bps aggregate drag): 29\% of
trades fire on route-direction mismatches at $-38.7\bps$ each.
(3)~\emph{Sizing error} (40--100\,bps): trading above the retail ceiling
($>\$50$K) walks the book beyond profitable depth.
Timing is the dominant term.
Cross-mechanism transfer (§\ref{sec:diagnostics}) and RL-based execution
policies that act on the first stress signal are the clearest paths
toward closing the gap.

\tp{Data availability.}
The repository contains all CSVs, figures, YAML configs, and Python
pipeline code.
The Binance Vision public archive covers calm-control and SVB windows.
Route-complete L2 for all legs requires a Tardis subscription and is
not committed to the repository.
