\title{Stablecoin StressBench: An Execution-Aware Benchmark\\
       for Stablecoin Dislocations}

%% ---- Abstract -----------------------------------------------
\begin{abstract}
Quoted stablecoin price gaps are not trades: we call dislocations
where a quoted basis exceeds a threshold \emph{optical}, reserving
\emph{executable} for the subset where net profit survives VWAP
slippage, taker fees, and settlement latency after routing through
a live order book.
Building \textbf{Stablecoin StressBench} on the March~2023 USDC/SVB
event, we find that 34.3\% of minutes show a $>10\bps$ optical
dislocation but only 2.88\% are executable: a \textbf{12$\times$
price-to-execution gap} (block bootstrap 95\,\% CI:
$8\times$--$24\times$), with no calm-trained model closing it and
the hindsight oracle exceeding $+225\bps$ net on the executable
arbitrage task.
Cross-mechanism meta-labeling trained on Terra/LUNA achieves
$+82.5\bps$ on the SVB test split (50.8\% of oracle returns),
demonstrating that order-book execution microstructure transfers
across structurally distinct stress mechanisms.
A three-way RL diagnostic isolates why: unconditioned PPO-GRU
degenerates to NoTrade at 2.3\% positive density; conditioned
PPO-GRU at 17\% density trades 919 times but earns $-29.2\bps$;
only meta-labeling's explicit positive-label supervision reaches
$+82.5\bps$---establishing that density is necessary but not
sufficient.
\end{abstract}

\keywords{stablecoin arbitrage, market microstructure,
  execution-aware labels, financial benchmarks, cryptocurrency}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

During stablecoin stress events, peg fragmentation creates cross-venue
price gaps that appear to be arbitrage opportunities.
In practice, VWAP slippage, taker fees, and CEX-to-CEX settlement
latency jointly eliminate most apparent profit.
We call dislocations where the quoted basis exceeds a threshold
\emph{optical}, and those where net profit survives execution costs
\emph{executable}.

Prior machine-learning work on stablecoin predictability uses
price-based labels to define ``profitable'' windows, which conflates
the optical and executable layers and systematically overstates how
predictable actual trading opportunities are.
\textbf{Stablecoin StressBench} is designed to close this gap.
It provides per-minute execution-aware labels that separate three
dislocation layers: (1) an \emph{optical} dislocation (quoted basis
exceeds a threshold), (2) an \emph{executable} dislocation (net profit
is positive after VWAP execution, fees, and settlement at a specified
notional), and (3) a \emph{predictable} dislocation (a model correctly
identifies the window ex ante).
The distance between these layers is the paper's core measurement.

We build the benchmark on the March 2023 USDC/SVB stress event, where
Silicon Valley Bank's collapse triggered a brief USDC depeg.
Using Binance L2 order-book data\footnote{The BTC-USDC buy leg uses
kline-proxy depth for the SVB window; all other legs use real L2.
The 20\% depth haircut sensitivity in \S\ref{sec:gap} bounds this
approximation.} and a full VWAP book-walking model, we find that
34.3\% of 1-minute windows show a $>10\bps$ price basis but only
2.88\% remain net-profitable at \$10K notional: a 12$\times$
price-to-execution gap.
The headline result is robust to buy-leg proxy error: a 20\%
depth haircut moves the executable rate from 2.88\% to 2.40\%
and widens the ratio from $12\times$ to $14\times$, with the oracle
remaining above $+130\bps$.
We then construct a model ladder spanning rule-based thresholds,
statistical baselines, gradient-boosted classifiers, sequence models
(GRU), and meta-labeling filters, evaluating each model's ability to
identify the 2.88\% of executable windows ex ante.
Every model trained on calm-control data loses money on executable
arbitrage tasks; the hindsight oracle gap exceeds 260\,bps.
Cross-mechanism meta-labeling, trained on a different stress event
(Terra/LUNA 2022), is the only approach that achieves positive net
returns ($+82.5\bps$, 50.8\% oracle capture).

\tp{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=1pt,topsep=1pt]
  \item \textbf{Execution-aware benchmark and labels:} per-minute
        labels with documented order-book depth provenance, VWAP
        book-walking, fees, and a hindsight oracle ceiling;
        accompanied by an 18-event stress taxonomy (seven mechanism
        classes, 2020--2023) that functions as data-tier governance
        specifying permitted claims per event, and an oracle gap
        decomposition into FP cost, timing, and sizing components.
        (\S\ref{sec:labels}, \S\ref{sec:historical};
         Tables~\ref{tab:gap}, \ref{tab:tiers}, \ref{tab:oracle})
  \item \textbf{Cross-mechanism transfer:} meta-labeling trained on
        Terra/LUNA achieves $+82.5\bps$ (50.8\% oracle capture) on
        the SVB test split---to our knowledge the first demonstration
        that order-book execution microstructure transfers across
        structurally distinct stress mechanisms, on the single
        cross-mechanism pair available with route-complete test data.
        (\S\ref{sec:diagnostics}, Table~\ref{tab:metalabel})
  \item \textbf{RL diagnostic---density necessary, supervision
        sufficient:} conditioned PPO-GRU at 17\% positive density
        earns $-29.2\bps$ vs.\ meta-labeling's $+82.5\bps$ at
        identical density, isolating explicit positive-label
        supervision as the additional requirement beyond RL reward
        optimisation.
        (\S\ref{sec:rl_results}, Table~\ref{tab:rl_results})
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\tp{Stablecoin stress.}
Uhlig \cite{uhlig2022luna} analyses the Terra/LUNA collapse, which
we use as a cross-mechanism validation split: the algorithmic
redemption spiral provides a structurally different stress trigger
than the SVB bank shock, making it a clean out-of-distribution test
for cross-mechanism transfer in execution microstructure.
Griffin and Shams \cite{griffin2020tether} find that quoted prices
can reflect non-fundamental demand, informing our decision to separate
optical from executable dislocations.
Hernandez Cruz et al.\ \cite{hernandezcruz2024svb} study USDC-USDT
DEX liquidity during the SVB collapse, motivating our execution-layer
analysis.

\tp{Limits to arbitrage and crypto fragmentation.}
Shleifer and Vishny \cite{shleifer1997limits} and Gromb and Vayanos
\cite{gromb2010limits} establish that arbitrage is limited by capital
constraints and execution frictions; we operationalise this at the
per-minute label level.
Makarov and Schoar \cite{makarov2020trading} show that cross-venue
crypto price gaps are not executable profits once latency and depth
are modelled, motivating our 12$\times$ price-to-execution gap.
Hautsch et al.\ \cite{hautsch2018blockchain} quantify settlement
latency as a first-order cost; we implement this as the fixed
$\delta{=}5\bps$ penalty in Equation~1.
Cont and Kukanov \cite{cont2013optimal} establish VWAP execution
price as the correct profitability object; our book-walking model
in \S\ref{sec:netlabels} implements this directly.
Gogol et al.\ \cite{gogol2024amm} introduce a Maximal Arbitrage
Value (MAV) between a USDC-ETH AMM pool and Binance, the closest
prior analogue to our oracle ceiling; our oracle extends this to
route-level CEX simulation, stress-event dislocations, and
model-evaluation benchmarking.

\tp{Financial ML and benchmarks.}
L\'{o}pez de Prado \cite{lopezdeprado2018} introduces meta-labeling
as a technique for filtering a primary signal with a secondary model
that learns when the primary signal produces true versus false positives.
We adopt this architecture in \S\ref{sec:diagnostics}, using a
LightGBM secondary model conditioned on primary-signal fires to learn
the microstructure signature of executable windows.
The cross-mechanism variant, training on Terra/LUNA and evaluating on
SVB, extends meta-labeling to out-of-distribution stress events, which
to our knowledge has not been done before.
Cintra and Holloway \cite{cintra2023bocpd} apply BOCPD
\cite{adamsmackay2007bocpd} to detect the Terra depeg 12~hours early
from Curve AMM pool reserves.
We test BOCPD as a stress-regime pre-filter and find AUROC~0.23 on
CEX cross-quote data: AMM reserves accumulate gradually, but CEX basis
is mean-reverting, explaining why their result does not transfer.
Cintra and Holloway also demonstrate cross-event transfer (2022 UST
training, March 2023 USDC depeg), but via AMM reserve depletion
signals rather than order-book execution features, with no oracle
evaluation.
Moallemi and Wang \cite{moallemi2022rl} and Ning et al.\
\cite{ning2021ddql} apply RL to optimal order execution in equity
markets, establishing the finite-horizon MDP framing we adopt in
\S\ref{sec:rl_baseline}.
Mohl et al.\ \cite{jaxmarlhft2024} train IPPO on roughly 400~million
real limit-order-book orders for a two-agent execution-and-market-making
setup, showing GPU-scale multi-agent RL is feasible on production LOB
data.
Olby et al.\ \cite{rightplace2024} build a reactive Simudyne simulator
that decomposes slippage into market impact and execution risk,
evaluating RL timing policies against an Almgren--Chr\'iss efficient
frontier; their reactive environment is the closest prior analogue to
the setting our RL diagnostic targets.
StressBench contributes the complementary finding: in a non-reactive
historical-replay setting with structurally rare positive labels,
density and explicit positive-label supervision are the binding
constraints---a pre-condition any reactive extension must satisfy.
No prior stablecoin-stress benchmark jointly provides one-minute
executable labels, route-level depth and fee/settlement modelling,
a hindsight profit ceiling, and cross-mechanism transfer evaluation.
Gogol et al.\ \cite{gogol2024amm} bound AMM arbitrage without
benchmark labels; Cintra and Holloway \cite{cintra2023bocpd} transfer
AMM depletion signals, not executable microstructure; Adams et al.\
\cite{adams2023mev} measure slippage without oracle evaluation; and
Wu and Liu \cite{wu2026spillovers} study spillovers, not execution
simulation. Stablecoin StressBench is the first to combine all five.

\section{Benchmark Design}
\label{sec:benchmark}

\subsection{Data Sources and Instruments}
\label{sec:data}

The final dataset contains 56,134 1-minute bars across six windows
(Table~\ref{tab:dataset}).
Price features come from Binance Vision (klines, aggTrades) and Coinbase
REST candles.
Net-profit labels use Binance BTC-USDC and BTC-USDT depth plus Coinbase
BTC-USD as the reference leg.

Every depth record is tagged with a provenance field
(Table~\ref{tab:route_provenance}).
The sell leg (BTC-USDT) uses real L2 futures bookDepth for all 2023--2024
windows.
The cross leg (USDC-USDT) uses real L2 from 2023-03-12 onward.
The buy leg (BTC-USDC) uses kline-proxy for the SVB window (BTC-USDC
perpetual did not exist on Binance until 2024) and real L2 for 2024
windows.
To bound this proxy error, we apply a 20\% depth haircut to the
kline-proxy buy leg: the executable rate falls from 2.88\% to 2.40\%,
the ratio widens from $12\times$ to $14\times$, and the oracle stays
above $+130\bps$. The headline result is robust to buy-leg proxy error.

\subsection{Event-Based Split Design}
\label{sec:splits}

Splits are defined by event identity (Figure~\ref{fig:basis}).
No model trained on the train split has seen stress dynamics;
threshold calibration on validation uses an independent stress event.
Labels are constructed by aligning each bar's outcome to the
observation window ending at $t{+}h$, with explicit checks confirming
no future information leaks into the input features.

\subsection{Historical Stress Taxonomy: Data-Tier Governance}
\label{sec:historical}

The 18-event taxonomy (2020--2023) is a benchmark governance
artefact: it specifies which events carry sufficient data quality
for execution-grade claims, and which are restricted to price
magnitude estimates or mechanism classification only.
It is shipped with the benchmark to foreclose over-interpretation
of results, not as an empirical finding in its own right.
Seven mechanism classes are covered: \emph{algorithmic/reflexive}
(FEI, IRON/TITAN, MIM, UST, USDD); \emph{fiat-reserve bank shock}
(USDC/SVB); \emph{regulatory winddown} (BUSD); \emph{exchange credit}
(Celsius/3AC, HUSD, FTX \cite{vidalthomas2023ftx}); \emph{DeFi pool
imbalance} (Curve/UST, USDT/Curve, USDC/DAI); \emph{collateral
liquidation} (DAI Black Thursday); \emph{RWA/niche} (Acala~aUSD, USDR).

Data availability governs permitted claims (Table~\ref{tab:tiers}).
Tier-A events (USDC/SVB test) support execution-gap, oracle-gap,
and model evaluation claims.
Tier-B ($N{=}11$, OHLCV/pool data) support price magnitude estimates
only.
Tier-C ($N{=}5$, partial/DEX-only) support mechanism taxonomy only.

\section{Execution-Aware Labels}
\label{sec:labels}

Labels operationalise the three-layer framework introduced in \S\ref{sec:intro}.
The price-to-execution gap measures Layer~1 divided by Layer~2.
The oracle gap measures Layer~3 against Layer~2 in hindsight.
This section details label construction.

\subsection{Cross-Quote Basis and Labels}
\label{sec:basis}

\[
  b_{\text{USDC}}(t) = 10{,}000 \times
    \frac{P_{\text{BTC/USDC}}(t) - P_{\text{BTC/USD}}(t)}
         {P_{\text{BTC/USD}}(t)} \quad [\text{bps}]
\]
$b_{\max} = \max(|b_{\text{USDC}}|, |b_{\text{USDT}}|)$.
Basis label: $y_{\text{basis}}(t) = \mathbf{1}[|b_{\text{USDC}}(t{+}h)| > \tau]$.
Primary task: \task{basis\_usdc\_1m\_gt10bps}.

\subsection{VWAP Execution and Net Profit Labels}
\label{sec:netlabels}

\begin{align}
  \text{net}(q,t) &= 10{,}000 \times
    \left[\frac{\text{VWAP}_{\text{sell}} - \text{VWAP}_{\text{buy}}}{P_{\text{ref}}}
    - f_{\text{buy}} - f_{\text{sell}} - \delta_{\text{settle}}\right]
    \quad [\text{bps}]\\
  y_{\text{arb}}(q,h,t) &= \mathbf{1}\!\left[
    \max_{s \in [t,\,t+h]} \text{net}(q,s) > c \right]
\end{align}

$P_{\text{ref}}$ is the Coinbase BTC-USD reference price;
$f_{\text{buy}}, f_{\text{sell}}, \delta_{\text{settle}}$ are
dimensionless fractions (e.g., 4\,bps taker fee $\Rightarrow
f_{\text{buy}} = 0.0004$).
Parameters: $c{=}10\bps$, taker fee 4\,bps/side, $\delta{=}5\bps$.
Primary economic task: \task{executable\_arb\_q10000\_5m}.
The oracle trades every minute where $\text{net}(q,t) > c$ in
hindsight, setting the performance ceiling no deployable model can
exceed.

\section{Models and Evaluation}
\label{sec:models}

\subsection{Model Ladder}
\label{sec:stack}

The ladder tests whether stronger models close the oracle gap, with
each rung testing a distinct hypothesis.
Threshold rules test whether price signals alone suffice.
ML classifiers test whether microstructure conditioning helps.
The expected-net-profit regressor tests whether the gap is a
label-mismatch artefact.
Sequence models test whether temporal depth patterns are exploitable.
Cross-mechanism meta-labeling tests whether stress microstructure
transfers across mechanism classes.

\tp{Economic anchors.} \texttt{NoTrade} ($0$ net bps) and
\texttt{Oracle} (hindsight ceiling).

\tp{Rule baselines.} \texttt{PriceBasis\{10,25\}bps} and
\texttt{GrossArbThreshold}: threshold rules on price data.

\tp{Statistical baselines.} \texttt{LastValue}, \texttt{RollingMean},
\texttt{AR1}: basis autocorrelation only.

\tp{ML classifiers.} Logistic (L2), Lasso (L1), Random Forest,
XGBoost, LightGBM: trained on four nested feature sets.

\tp{Expected-net-profit regressor.} Directly regresses
$\hat{y}=\text{net}(q,t)$ and trades when $\hat{y}>\theta^*$.
If this fails, the gap is structural, not label-mismatch.

\tp{Meta-labeling filter.} Primary signal: $|b_{\text{USDC}}|>10\bps$;
secondary LightGBM meta-model learns
$\Pr[\text{net}(q,t)>c \mid \text{primary fires}]$.

\tp{Regime detectors.} EWMA z-score, CUSUM, BOCPD as two-stage
stress-regime pre-filters.

\tp{Sequence models.} GRU and MLP-Window classifiers trained on
30-minute sliding windows of order-book microstructure features
(\S\ref{sec:seq_results}).

\subsection{RL Execution Policy Baseline}
\label{sec:rl_baseline}

We frame the entry-timing problem as a finite-horizon MDP.
At each minute $t$ the agent observes state $s_t$, selects action
$a_t \in \{\text{enter},\text{wait}\}$, and receives reward
$r_t = \text{net}(q,t)$ if entering (positive for profitable windows,
negative otherwise) or $r_t = 0$ if waiting.
The state $s_t$ is the 30-minute look-back window of
\texttt{price\_plus\_book} features, the smallest feature set that
delivers meaningful microstructure lift over price-only signals
(see \S\ref{sec:shap} for SHAP evidence).
This feature set is available in real time without
settlement-latency dependency.
We train a PPO agent with a GRU policy network (one hidden layer,
64 units, 30-step look-back) using the cross-mechanism protocol:
training on Terra/LUNA stress windows only, evaluation on the USDC/SVB
test split without retraining.
This directly tests whether the sequential timing framing resolves
the dominant gap component identified in \S\ref{sec:decomp}.

\subsection{Calibration and Evaluation}
\label{sec:eval}

Threshold calibration on validation split:
$\theta^* = \arg\max_\theta \sum_{i:\hat{p}_i>\theta} \text{net}(q,t_i)$
subject to $\geq 25$ trades.
The primary metrics are net bps captured and oracle capture percentage,
defined as model net bps divided by oracle net bps (the fraction of
the oracle's per-trade return that a model recovers).
Secondary metrics are AUROC and AUPRC.
\texttt{NoTrade} at $0\bps$ is the economic floor.

Three claim boundaries apply: (1) the BTC-USDC buy leg uses
kline-proxy depth for the SVB window; (2) oracle-gap results apply to
the SVB test split ($N{=}15{,}832$) only; (3) cost parameters are
$c{=}10\bps$, 4\,bps taker fee per side, $\delta{=}5\bps$.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Price Gaps Are Common, Execution Is Rare}
\label{sec:gap}

At 10\,bps, 34.3\% of test minutes exceed the max-basis threshold;
only 2.88\% yield net profit, a \textbf{12$\times$ price-to-execution
ratio} (block bootstrap 95\,\% CI: $8\times$--$24\times$,
$B{=}10{,}000$, 60-min blocks).
The gap persists at all tested thresholds (Table~\ref{tab:gap}) and
is robust across cost regimes: at the worst-case setting
(10\,bps settlement, high fees), the executable rate falls to 2.40\%
and the ratio widens to 14$\times$.
At \$50K notional the executable rate falls to 1.62\%; at \$500K it
reaches 0.03\%, confirming that book depth, not fees, prices out
institutional participants.
At institutional scale, book depth rather than fees is the binding
constraint (0.03\% executable rate at \$500K), consistent with the
depth-based limits-to-arbitrage arguments in \S\ref{sec:related}.

\subsection{Cross-Mechanism Transfer: Benchmark Generalisation Evidence}
\label{sec:diagnostics}

\tp{Meta-labeling result.}
Calm-control training yields zero meta-positives (0\% positive rate),
so we retrain on Terra/LUNA (1{,}559 primary fires, 265 meta-positives,
17.0\% rate) and evaluate cross-mechanism on SVB.
All fitting and threshold selection use Terra/LUNA only; no SVB labels
are observed before final evaluation.
With \texttt{price\_plus\_book} features the filter achieves $+82.5\bps$
(397 trades, 50.8\% oracle capture) vs.\ zero with calm training
(Table~\ref{tab:metalabel}).
This is consistent with execution microstructure that transfers
across stress mechanisms: the same order-book features that predict
profitable windows during an algorithmic collapse (Terra/LUNA) also
predict them during a fiat-reserve shock (SVB).
The meta-model's dominant features mirror the main SHAP ranking:
ask-side depth at 10\,bps and bid-ask spread are the leading
microstructure signals in both calm-trained LightGBM and the
Terra-trained meta-model secondary classifier.
The structural explanation: CEX market makers withdraw depth under
uncertainty regardless of trigger, because the cause is opaque at
the order-book timescale, making depth withdrawal a consistent
cross-mechanism precursor whose feature signature transfers while
the magnitude differs by event
(5.9$\times$ Terra/LUNA vs.\ 12$\times$ SVB).
We are not aware of prior work demonstrating cross-mechanism transfer
for execution-aware stablecoin models.

\subsubsection*{6.5.1\enspace Regime detectors and false-positive diagnosis}

\tp{Regime detectors.}
CUSUM achieves near-perfect recall (0.9995) at 83.5\% false alarm rate;
EWMA achieves 86.9\% accuracy at 0.65\% FAR with 0.95\% recall;
BOCPD achieves AUROC~0.229 on CEX cross-quote data.
This last result contrasts with Cintra and Holloway
\cite{cintra2023bocpd}, who found BOCPD detected the Terra depeg
12~hours early from Curve pool reserves.
The difference is the signal: AMM reserves reflect LP withdrawal, which
accumulates gradually, while CEX cross-quote basis is noisier and
mean-reverting, making changepoint detection unreliable on CEX prices.

\tp{False-positive structural diagnosis.}
\texttt{PriceBasis10bps} produces 2,002 trades; 581 (29\%) are FPs
at mean $-38.7\bps$.
The cause is route-direction mismatch: true-positive windows have a
USDC basis of 344\,bps (genuine discount), while FP windows have
0.56\,bps---triggered by USDT dislocations where the USDC route is
not simultaneously profitable.
FP windows show \emph{higher} bid depth ($72{,}805$ vs.\ $59{,}961$
BTC units), ruling out thin books; the failure mode is direction, not
depth.
LightGBM's 33\% hit rate (vs.\ 6\%) shows microstructure conditioning
partially diagnoses mismatch, but 67\% FP remains a timing gap.

\tp{SHAP feature importance.}
\label{sec:shap}
SHAP analysis of LightGBM shows price features carrying 94.8\% of
total importance; ask-side depth at 10\,bps and bid-ask spread
contribute just 3.4\% combined.
The implication is causal: a calm-trained model learns calm price
dynamics, not stress execution structure---the microstructure
features are present in the input but contribute negligibly because
the calm training split contains no profitable stress windows from
which the model could learn their relevance.
This is why the oracle gap is structural rather than a label-mismatch
artefact: the features needed to identify executable windows are
available but the training distribution renders them uninformative.
Cross-mechanism training on Terra/LUNA is the fix: the meta-model
sees stress microstructure at 17\% positive density and learns the
depth-withdrawal signature that calm training cannot, tying the model
ladder, oracle gap, and transfer result into a single causal account.

\subsection{Oracle Gap Decomposition}
\label{sec:decomp}

The 260\,bps gap on the executable arbitrage task decomposes into
three components (component~1 derived directly from test-split
results; components~2--3 estimated from the executable-rate
sensitivity analysis).
First, false-positive cost: for \texttt{PriceBasis10bps}, 581 FP
trades at $-38.7\bps$ each produce $-22{,}484\bps$ of total losses,
the dominant quantifiable drag for any threshold-based strategy.
Second, timing error (estimated 40--80\,bps): entering 1--2 minutes
late shifts execution to a lower-depth regime, and the
depth-withdrawal signature in the 30-minute pre-window is the signal
a future model must capture earlier.
Third, sizing error (estimated 40--100\,bps): the executable rate
falls from 2.88\% at \$10K to 1.62\% at \$50K, pricing out 44\% of
windows above the retail ceiling.
Closing the gap requires cross-mechanism stress training combined with
earlier timing entry, not larger notional.

paper's sharpest architectural finding.

explicit supervision as additionally required.

The unconditioned PPO-GRU converges to degenerate NoTrade: it makes
zero entries despite AUROC~0.74.
The 2.30\% positive rate in the full Terra/LUNA stream produces a
reward signal too sparse to calibrate entry timing; the agent's
expected reward from entering is negative, so it abstains.

A conditioned PPO-GRU, trained only on primary-signal windows
($|b_{\text{USDC}}|{>}10\bps$, mirroring the meta-label training
distribution at 17\% positive rate), \emph{does} trade:
919 entries, AUROC~0.87, net $-29.2\bps$ (Table~\ref{tab:rl_results}).
This rules out training sparsity as the sole failure mode.
The conditioned RL agent avoids degenerate NoTrade but cannot match
meta-labeling ($+82.5\bps$, 111.7\,bps gap), indicating that
\emph{explicit positive-label supervision} (which meta-labeling
provides via the secondary classifier) outperforms RL reward
optimisation at the same training density.
The mechanism is qualitative: the meta-labeling classifier receives
direct binary supervision ($y{=}1$ for profitable windows), whereas
the RL agent must infer the profitable subset through a reward signal
negative on 83\% of entries---the supervision is categorically
cleaner, not just denser.
The 919-trade overtrading pattern (vs.\ the oracle's 315) is
diagnostic: policies failing from insufficient training converge to
NoTrade, not systematic false-positive entry, suggesting a
timing-transfer mismatch rather than a fundamental RL limitation.
Multi-mechanism training is the productive next step.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

\tp{Data scope.}
The BTC-USDC buy leg uses kline-proxy depth for the SVB window; the
20\% haircut sensitivity confirms robustness.
Extending Tier-A coverage to AMM or exchange-credit events requires
route-complete L2 archives for those venues.

\tp{Model scope.}
The model ladder covers tabular classifiers, sequence models, and
cross-mechanism PPO-GRU variants.
The conditioned RL result ($-29.2\bps$, 919 trades) may reflect
overfitting to Terra/LUNA's specific depth-withdrawal timing rather
than a fundamental failure of sequential optimisation; resolving this
requires additional cross-mechanism pairs or longer training runs.
Positive example density and explicit positive-label supervision are
jointly necessary for positive net returns (\S\ref{sec:rl_results}).

\tp{Market-impact feedback.}
Unlike reactive-simulator approaches such as Olby et al.\
\cite{rightplace2024}, in which agents
observe how their own order flow consumes book depth, the PPO-GRU
trains on historical replay and cannot model this feedback loop.
In a thin-book stress regime this systematically miscalibrates
entry sizing: the agent observes book depth as it was, not as it
would be after its own orders clear liquidity.
This is a candidate explanation for the 919-trade overtrading
pattern observed at identical training density to meta-labeling.
Reactive-simulator training is the productive next step for closing
the timing component of the oracle gap, combining the cross-mechanism
initialisation protocol shown here with an environment that updates
depth in response to agent actions.


%% =============================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

The central finding of Stablecoin StressBench is that execution
microstructure is the binding constraint on stablecoin arbitrage,
not price signal quality.
A 34.3\% optical dislocation rate collapses to 2.88\% once order-book
depth and transaction costs are applied.
No model trained on calm-market data closes this gap. Cross-mechanism
training on a different stress event is the empirically validated path
to positive net returns ($+82.5\bps$, 50.8\% oracle capture).
No calm-control-trained model earns positive returns on the executable
arbitrage task; LightGBM ($+17.2\bps$, basis task only) is the sole
non-oracle positive result, 145\,bps below the oracle ceiling.
The oracle gap decomposition (\S\ref{sec:decomp}) identifies
false-positive cost as the dominant quantifiable drag and timing as
the primary structural barrier.

The RL experiments (\S\ref{sec:rl_results}) sharpen the benchmark
implication: \textbf{positive example density is necessary but not
sufficient; explicit supervision of the positive subset is the
additional requirement.}
The unconditioned PPO-GRU degenerates (2.3\% density too sparse).
The conditioned PPO-GRU trained on the same 17\% primary-signal
windows as the meta-label does trade ($-29.2\bps$, 919 trades) but
cannot match meta-labeling ($+82.5\bps$), leaving an 111.7\,bps gap
attributable to meta-labeling's explicit positive-label supervision
rather than RL reward optimisation.

\tp{Benchmark deliverables.}
The anonymised repository (URL redacted for review) ships five
concrete artefacts: (1)~per-minute executable labels for all six
benchmark windows; (2)~the VWAP book-walking label-construction
code with route-level depth provenance; (3)~the four nested feature
sets (\texttt{price\_only} through \texttt{price\_book\_settle});
(4)~the hindsight oracle implementation and full model-ladder
evaluation harness; and (5)~the cross-mechanism meta-labeling and
PPO-GRU training scripts.
Calm-control and SVB windows are reproducible from the public Binance
Vision archive; route-complete L2 requires a Tardis subscription.
